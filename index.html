• WHAT THIS IS AND WHY IT'S DIFFERENT
Every drum machine and sequencer that exists right now is deterministic. You program a pattern, you press play, it plays back the same thing. Every time. That's been true since the first drum machines in the 1970s — and it's still true of every DAW, every groovebox, every hardware sequencer made today.

Q-ACID 303 isn't deterministic. It uses ideas from quantum mechanics to make patterns that are fundamentally non-repeatable. Not "random" in the way a dice roll is random — something weirder.

The short version: In quantum physics, particles don't have a definite state until you measure them. Before measurement, they exist in a superposition — a blend of all possible states at once. The act of looking forces them to "pick one." That's called wave function collapse. And when two particles are entangled, measuring one instantly determines the other, no matter how far apart they are.

Those aren't metaphors here. They're the actual rules the sequencer runs on. A step in superposition doesn't have a value yet — it contains all possible values simultaneously, weighted by probability. Each time the loop cycles, it collapses into something. Next cycle, it collapses into something else. The pattern literally doesn't exist in a fixed form until you listen to it.

Why that matters musically: Normal electronic music is a recording of a decision. This is more like a recording of a probability. Two people listening to the same pattern at the same time would hear different things. An exported WAV captures one specific collapse — a snapshot of one possibility out of an astronomical number. It won't happen again.

3 DRUM MACHINE — 7 TRACKS
16 or 32-step sequencer with seven independent drum tracks. Each has its own pattern, velocity, quantum state, and mute/solo. Kick, snare and hi-hat are visible by default. Add the rest via the "+ ADD TRACK" buttons at the bottom of the drum panel.

KICK — Saturated sine sweep (180→34Hz) + square click + sub punch. Steps 1, 5, 9, 13 for classic four-on-the-floor.

SNARE — Two-band noise + detuned triangle oscillators. 909-style crack. Try steps 5 and 13 for a backbeat.

CLAP — Three quick bursts + reverberant tail. Classic 909 design. Pairs well with or replaces the snare.

HAT — Six inharmonic squares through bandpass (808-style metallic). Offbeats and 16th rolls add movement.

OPEN HAT — Same engine as hat with a longer tail. Great on offbeats against closed hats.

RIDE — Bell harmonics (4400Hz + harmonics) + metallic noise sustain. Downbeat ride bells push tracks forward.

PERC — Low tom / floor thud. Body + noise attack. Good for fills and syncopated accents.

Click a step to toggle. M mutes, S solos, × hides the track and clears its pattern. White outline = playhead.
4 303 ACID LINE
Monophonic bass sequencer with authentic TB-303-inspired engine.

1. Pick a note from the buttons below the grid (C2–G3)
2. Click a step to place it, click again to remove

Accent — Louder, brighter, more resonant, longer decay. Same behaviour as the original 303.
Slide — Legato portamento into the next note with no envelope retrigger. Essential for acid lines.
Glide mode — Global toggle making every note glide from the previous one. Try it on dense bass lines.
Key / Octave — Transpose the whole pattern without rewriting notes.
5 ACID SYNTH ENGINE
Upgraded to a cascaded two-pole filter design with pre-filter saturation for authentic 303 squelch:

Waveform: SAW (buzzy, classic 303), SQR (hollower, thicker), TRI (softer), SIN (clean sub).

Cutoff — Filter frequency. Low = dark and muffled. High = bright and open.
Reso — Resonance at the cutoff peak. Higher = more squelchy whistle. Above 22 starts self-oscillating.
Env Mod — How much the filter sweeps per note. This is the main "acid character" knob.
Drive — Pre-filter tanh saturation. Adds warmth, harmonics, and grit before the filter. Try 30–60 for classic tones.
Attack — Envelope rise time. Keep low (1–5ms) for punchy 303 feel.
Decay — Filter envelope decay length. Short = blippy. Long = swoopy. Accent extends this by ~40%.
Release — Amplitude tail after the decay. Shorter sounds more staccato.
Accent — Global accent depth. Per-step accents boost cutoff, resonance, decay and volume together.
Sub Vol — Sub-oscillator (1 octave down, sine) mixed before the filter. Adds low-end weight.
Glide — Portamento time. Lower = snappy slides, higher = swoopy slides.
Starting point for classic acid
Cutoff: 400–800 · Reso: 18–24 · Env Mod: 75–90 · Drive: 30–55 · Decay: 80–150 · SAW · Glide ON — tweak from there

7 QUANTUM ENGINE
Instead of steps being just ON or OFF, they can exist in superposition — kind of both at once, resolving differently each loop.

How to use: Hit the ⟨ψ⟩ Quantum toggle on a section, then click steps — they go purple. Each loop, those steps randomly decide whether to fire or not.

Core operations:
Superpose All — Every step goes quantum with uniform phase. Full uncertainty.
⟨ψ| Collapse — Locks all quantum steps into fixed values. That particular pattern probably won't happen again (odds around 10⁻²⁸).
Q-Fill — Adds quantum states to empty steps only, leaves your programmed ones alone.
Entangle Kick↔Bass — Creates a Bell |Φ+⟩ state. Kick hit → bass fires; kick suppressed on an entangled step → bass suppressed too. Real quantum correlation.
Randomize — Fresh pattern using your probability weights.
Mutate — Small random tweaks to what's already there.

Quantum gates (advanced):
H (Hadamard) — Puts every step into equal superposition with random phases. Different from Superpose All because phase affects how collapse biases — with phases, interference emerges.
X (Pauli-X) — Bitflip gate. Inverts every classical ON/OFF state, leaves quantum states alone. Good for quickly inverting a pattern.
⟨Zeno⟩ — Activates the quantum Zeno effect. Continuous observation pins quantum states open — superposed steps mostly stay silent while the toggle is on, creating sparse ghostly variations.
8 DECOHERENCE
In actual quantum physics, superposition breaks down over time as things interact with their environment. That's decoherence.

Turn it on and quantum steps gradually lose coherence each loop. They go from purple (quantum) to pink (decohering) and eventually collapse into fixed hits. So the pattern sort of starts loose and crystallizes into something solid over time.

Rate — How fast coherence degrades per loop.
Threshold — When the visual transition to "decohering" kicks in.
9 PROBABILITY WEIGHTS
Real quantum systems don't have uniform probabilities. The grid lets you weight each note (C–B) from 1 to 5. Heavier = shows up more when quantum steps collapse.

Scale presets: Minor, Pentatonic, Blues, Chromatic, Equal. Helps keep things sounding musical even when the quantum engine is doing its thing.

10 WORKFLOW IDEAS
One way to build something:
1. Load a template that's in the right ballpark
2. Set BPM (TAP to match something you're playing alongside)
3. Mess with Cutoff and Reso — they change the character the most
4. Q-Fill empty steps for variation that shifts each loop
5. Try Sidechain if you want the pumping feel
6. Decoherence is interesting for patterns that evolve on their own

≡ THE MATHS
The quantum state of a single sequencer step in Dirac notation:

|step⟩ = α|on⟩ + β|off⟩

α and β are complex probability amplitudes, |α|² + |β|² = 1. The probability of triggering on a given cycle is |α|². The instruments set these differently — kick has |α|² ≈ 0.6, snare ≈ 0.5, hat ≈ 0.65.

For bass notes, superposition extends across the note space:

|bass⟩ = Σᵢ cᵢ|noteᵢ⟩

The coefficients cᵢ are shaped by the probability weight grid. Setting weights to a minor scale concentrates amplitude on scale-degree notes, so collapses tend toward musically coherent results. Analogous to how a potential energy landscape determines which quantum states are more probable.

Probability of two 64-step collapses producing identical results across all four tracks:

P(identical) ≈ (0.6)¹⁶ × (0.5)¹⁶ × (0.65)¹⁶ × (1/7)¹⁶ ≈ 10⁻²⁸

About 10 billion times less likely than winning the lottery twice in a row.

Decoherence models the quantum-to-classical transition. In real systems, off-diagonal density matrix elements decay exponentially:

ρᵢⱼ(t) → ρᵢⱼ(0)e⁻ᵗ/τ

where τ is the decoherence time. Here, each loop increments a coherence counter per step. When it crosses a threshold, the step irreversibly collapses — modelling how superposition is lost through environmental coupling.

Entanglement between kick and bass produces non-separable correlations. A kick on step N forces a bass note on the same step, constrained to harmonically related intervals. The joint probability P(kick ∧ bass) ≠ P(kick) × P(bass) — the formal condition for entanglement.

This currently runs on pseudorandom number generation, not quantum hardware. The musical output is structurally the same either way — connecting to a real quantum backend (IBM Quantum, etc.) would make the randomness genuinely irreducible rather than algorithmically derived.
